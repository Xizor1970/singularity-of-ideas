The Graveyard of Ideas

Before we can define what makes an idea good, we must confront the graveyard. For every idea that thrives today, thousands have died. And the dead include ideas that were once supremely dominant — ideas that billions of people organised their lives around, fought wars over, and would have died to defend.

Dead Religions — Ideas That Once Ruled the World

The Greek pantheon dominated the Mediterranean for over a millennium. Zeus, Athena, and Apollo were not quaint myths — they were the operating system of civilisation. Temples were built. Wars were waged. Lives were structured around their worship. Then they died.

Egyptian religion: 3,000+ years of continuous worship. Ra, Osiris, Isis — ideas so dominant they built the pyramids. Dead.

Norse mythology: Odin, Thor, Valhalla — the framework that organised Scandinavian society for centuries. Dead (except as Marvel content).

Mithraism: Once the primary rival to Christianity in the Roman Empire. Temples across Europe. Millions of adherents. Dead — almost no trace remains.

Zoroastrianism: State religion of three successive Persian empires spanning 1,000 years. Influenced Judaism, Christianity, and Islam. Now fewer than 200,000 adherents worldwide.

Mayan religion: Organised an entire civilisation's architecture, calendar, agriculture, and politics for 2,000+ years. Dead — surviving only in fragments.

3,000+ years
Even ideas that dominated for three millennia can die. Longevity alone does not make an idea "good" — it makes it fit for its era.

Dead Scientific Ideas

It's not just religions. Scientific ideas die too:

Phlogiston theory (1667–1789): Dominated chemistry for 120 years. Every chemist in Europe believed combustion released an invisible substance called phlogiston. Replaced by oxygen theory.

Luminiferous aether (1700s–1905): Light must travel through a medium — obvious, intuitive, and wrong. Killed by Michelson-Morley and Einstein.

Miasma theory (ancient–1880s): Disease comes from bad air. Built entire public health systems around this idea (some accidentally useful — draining swamps did reduce malaria). Killed by germ theory.

Steady-state universe (1948–1965): The universe has no beginning — it has always existed. Elegant, philosophically attractive, and killed by the cosmic microwave background.

Behaviourism (1913–1960s): Dominated psychology for 50 years. Only observable behaviour matters; internal mental states are unscientific. Killed by the cognitive revolution.

Ideas are like species in the fossil record. For every living species today, thousands have gone extinct. The dinosaurs didn't die because they were "bad" organisms — they were supremely adapted to their era. They died because the environment changed. Dead ideas are the same: not inherently bad, but fatally adapted to environments that no longer exist.

The Unit Problem: What Even Is an Idea?

Before we can define what makes an idea good, we face a more fundamental question: what is the unit of analysis? What, precisely, are we measuring when we measure "an idea"?

The Bible Is Not an Idea

Consider the Bible. It has been copied, distributed, and referenced more than any other text in human history — over 5 billion copies printed, translated into 700+ languages, cited in countless legal systems, artworks, and philosophical arguments for two millennia.

But the Bible is not an idea. It is a container — a vessel holding hundreds of distinct ideas: monotheism, original sin, resurrection, the Golden Rule, covenant theology, eschatology, the concept of grace, the Sermon on the Mount, the Book of Revelation's apocalypticism. Some of these ideas are among the most influential ever conceived. Others are ignored by most of the Bible's own adherents. Some contradict each other.

The Bible has no single "citation count" because it is not a single idea. It is an idea-generating system — a matrix from which new interpretations, theologies, denominations, and moral frameworks continuously emerge. It has produced Quaker pacifism and the Crusades, liberation theology and the prosperity gospel, abolitionism and the justification of slavery. The same container; radically different ideas extracted from it.

A Scientific Paper Is Not an Idea Either

Shannon's 1948 paper has ~170,000 citations. But what is being cited? The paper contains dozens of distinct ideas: the bit as a unit of information, entropy as average surprise, the source coding theorem, the noisy-channel coding theorem, mutual information, redundancy. Each is a separate idea that has propagated differently, been applied in different fields, and achieved different levels of influence.

When we say "Shannon's paper is highly cited," we are measuring the container — not any specific idea within it. The citation count is a proxy, and a blurry one. A paper might be cited for its method, its dataset, its framing, a single equation, or just because it's famous enough that citing it signals credibility.

The most-cited paper in history — Lowry et al. (1951) on protein measurement, 305,000+ citations — is cited not for a deep idea but for a technique. The paper with the most intellectual influence (perhaps Darwin's Origin, or Newton's Principia) has far fewer formal citations because its ideas have been "obliterated by incorporation" — so thoroughly absorbed into the culture that citing the source is unnecessary.

The most powerful ideas have no citation count because they have no single source. Who do you cite for "democracy"? For "zero"? For "cause and effect"? The greatest ideas predate the systems we use to track influence.

Three Levels of Granularity

Ideas exist at multiple scales — and conflating them creates confusion

Level 1: The Atomic Idea — A single, irreducible insight that can be expressed in one sentence.

• "Species evolve through natural selection"

• "Energy equals mass times the speed of light squared"

• "Information is surprise"

• "Do unto others as you would have them do unto you"

These are the true units of intellectual evolution. Each is a single replicable entity — a meme in Dawkins's original sense. They can be evaluated for truth, tested for utility, measured for compression, and tracked for spread.

Level 2: The Container — A vessel that carries one or more atomic ideas.

• A scientific paper (carries 1–10 atomic ideas plus methodology and data)

• A book (carries dozens to hundreds of atomic ideas organised by narrative)

• A scripture (carries a civilisation's worth of atomic ideas accumulated over centuries)

• A GitHub repository (carries an architectural idea plus thousands of implementation decisions)

Containers are what we can count — citations, copies, downloads, stars. But counting containers is counting vehicles, not passengers. A book cited a million times might contain one great idea and 500 pages of filler. A book cited twice might contain the next paradigm shift — trapped in the wrong container.

Level 3: The Tradition / Paradigm / Memeplex — A self-reinforcing system of ideas that evolves as a unit.

• Christianity (monotheism + soteriology + ecclesiology + ethics + eschatology + …)

• Darwinism (natural selection + common descent + gradualism + sexual selection + …)

• Object-oriented programming (encapsulation + inheritance + polymorphism + …)

• Democracy (popular sovereignty + individual rights + separation of powers + …)

Traditions are living systems — they generate new atomic ideas, shed old ones, absorb ideas from competitors, and evolve over time. "Christianity" in 100 CE, 1500 CE, and 2025 CE share a family resemblance but are not the same memeplex. The tradition persists; its constituent ideas turn over.

The three levels are like biology's hierarchy. An atomic idea is a gene — a single unit of heritable information. A container is a chromosome — a physical structure carrying many genes, inherited as a bundle. A tradition is a species — a self-perpetuating lineage that maintains its identity over time even as individual genes come and go. You can study evolution at any level, but confusing them leads to nonsense. A gene doesn't have a lifespan; a species doesn't have a nucleotide sequence. Similarly: "democracy" doesn't have a publication date, and Shannon's paper doesn't have adherents.

The Measurement Problem

Why We Measure Containers When We Should Measure Ideas

We measure containers (papers, books, patents) because they are countable. Citation databases, sales figures, download counts — all track containers. But this creates systematic blind spots:

Blind spot 1: Ideas without containers. The most fundamental ideas — logic, causality, number, language — have no identifiable origin and therefore no citation count. They are so old and so universal that they predate all recording systems. These "atmospheric ideas" are arguably the most important of all — and they are invisible to any container-based measurement.

Blind spot 2: Ideas that outgrew their containers. Natural selection has long since "escaped" the Origin of Species. Nobody reads Darwin to learn evolution — they read textbooks that have incorporated, refined, and extended his ideas through 160 years of further work. The container is a historical artefact; the idea is alive elsewhere.

Blind spot 3: The Bible problem. Religious scriptures contain multitudes. Measuring "Bible influence" conflates thousands of distinct ideas with wildly different truth values, utility scores, and propagation histories. The Golden Rule and the endorsement of slavery are in the same container — but they are radically different ideas with radically different quality scores.

Blind spot 4: Ideas spread across many containers. "Machine learning" is not in one paper — it's in 500,000+ papers, thousands of libraries, millions of codebases. No single container captures it. The idea's influence vastly exceeds any individual container's citation count.

So What Should We Measure?

The Case for Measuring Atomic Ideas

If we want to understand idea dominance — what makes ideas succeed, persist, and deserve to persist — we should measure at the atomic level. Not "how many times was this paper cited?" but "how many minds hold this specific insight, and how has it changed their behaviour?"

This is harder to measure but more meaningful:

• "Natural selection drives adaptation" — held by ~2 billion educated adults, regardless of whether they've read Darwin. Influences medicine, agriculture, AI research, and business strategy daily.

• "Wash your hands to prevent disease transmission" — held by ~5+ billion people. Saves millions of lives per year. Semmelweis's container was rejected; his idea conquered the world.

• "There is one God" — held by ~4 billion people across three distinct traditions. No single citation. No single author. Possibly the most influential atomic idea in human history — yet unmeasurable by conventional bibliometrics.

The most important ideas are often the ones we can measure least. This is not a flaw in the ideas — it is a flaw in our measurement systems, which were designed to track containers, not content.

Speculation

Can AI Solve the Unit Problem?

AI might finally make it possible to measure ideas at the atomic level rather than the container level. An AI system could:

• Parse millions of papers, books, and social media posts to identify distinct atomic ideas (rather than just counting container citations)

• Track the same atomic idea across different containers, languages, and traditions — seeing "the Golden Rule" in the Bible, the Analects of Confucius, the Mahabharata, and secular ethics as one idea with multiple containers

• Measure idea influence by behavioural change rather than citation — "how many people's actions reflect this idea?" rather than "how many papers reference this paper?"

• Identify when two seemingly different traditions are actually carrying the same atomic idea in different packaging — and when two uses of the same word actually carry different ideas

This would be the first true "idea census" — measuring the actual population of ideas in human minds, rather than the number of times their containers have been counted.

Such a system does not yet exist. Current NLP can identify topic similarity but cannot reliably decompose a complex text into its constituent atomic ideas and track their independent propagation histories. This is a hard AI problem — arguably requiring genuine understanding, not just pattern matching.

The Problem with "Fitness = Good"

Why Survival Is Not a Sufficient Definition of Quality

Throughout this knowledge base, we have described the mechanisms of idea dominance — network effects, power laws, memetic fitness, compression. But we have avoided a crucial value judgment: is a dominant idea necessarily a good idea?

The dead religions prove the answer is no. They dominated for millennia. They were supremely fit. They are now dead — which means fitness is necessary but not sufficient for long-term survival, and survival is not the same as goodness.

Conversely, many ideas that we would call "good" (accurate, useful, important) have failed to spread:

Semmelweis (1847): Discovered that handwashing prevents childbed fever. Data was overwhelming. He was ignored, mocked, and died in an asylum. The idea was true, useful, and compressed — but it failed on fitness (it insulted doctors' self-image).

Wegener (1912): Continental drift — correct, supported by evidence, and rejected for 50 years because the mechanism (plate tectonics) wasn't yet discovered.

Boltzmann (1870s): Statistical mechanics and atomic theory — correct, but so far ahead of its time that Boltzmann was driven to suicide by professional rejection.

If fitness ≠ goodness, and truth ≠ dominance, then we need a definition of "good idea" that is independent of whether the idea currently dominates.

Four Dimensions of Idea Quality

We propose that a truly good idea — one that deserves dominance — is one that scores high on four independent dimensions simultaneously. Each dimension alone is insufficient. Only the combination defines genuine quality.

Dimension 1: Truth

Does the idea correspond to reality?

An idea is true if its claims match observable reality — if its predictions are confirmed, its descriptions are accurate, and its mechanisms actually operate as described.

Scores high alone: String theory (possibly true, possibly the deepest description of reality — but untestable, inaccessible, and unable to spread beyond a tiny elite). Many mathematical theorems are true but have zero cultural penetration.

Scores low: Astrology, phlogiston, flat earth, miasma. These ideas are false — their predictions fail, their mechanisms don't exist.

Tricky cases: Religion. Are religious claims "false"? Core metaphysical claims (God exists, the soul persists) are unfalsifiable — they are neither true nor false in the scientific sense. They occupy a different epistemic category entirely.

Dimension 2: Utility

Does the idea enable action and produce results?

A useful idea gives its holder capabilities they didn't have before — it lets them build, heal, predict, create, or solve problems they previously couldn't.

Scores high alone: Newtonian mechanics. Not the deepest truth (superseded by relativity and quantum mechanics) — but still supremely useful for engineering. We build bridges with Newton, not Einstein.

Scores low: Many philosophical ideas (the trolley problem, the ship of Theseus) are interesting but produce no practical capability.

Key insight: Utility and truth are correlated but not identical. An idea can be useful while being technically false (Newtonian mechanics at low speeds), and true while being useless (the exact mass of the Higgs boson doesn't help you build anything — yet).

Dimension 3: Fitness

Can the idea spread, persist, and defend itself?

A fit idea is one that people actually adopt, remember, share, and build upon. It is compressible enough to be communicated, compelling enough to be remembered, and structured enough to resist mutation.

Scores high alone: Conspiracy theories, viral misinformation, catchy slogans, urban legends. "5G causes COVID" spread to millions within weeks — supremely fit, completely false, and useless.

Scores low: Many true and useful ideas fail on fitness because they are complex, counterintuitive, or emotionally unappealing. Bayesian reasoning is true and useful but most people cannot apply it intuitively.

The dead religions scored here: The Greek pantheon was supremely fit for its era — vivid, narrative, emotionally resonant. It died when a fitter memeplex (Christianity — with better community formation, afterlife promises, and institutional infrastructure) outcompeted it.

Dimension 4: Compression

How much does the idea explain relative to its own complexity?

A compressed idea is one that captures deep structure — a short description that generates vast predictions. This is the information-theoretic measure of intellectual power.

Scores high alone: The Schrödinger equation. Extraordinary compression — one equation describing the behaviour of all quantum systems. But it cannot spread beyond physicists (low fitness for general population) and its "utility" requires enormous infrastructure to exploit.

Scores low: An encyclopaedia. Contains vast knowledge but has zero compression — it's just a list. No pattern, no generative power, no predictive capacity beyond what's explicitly stated.

Why compression matters: Compressed ideas are the ones most likely to be true in the Solomonoff sense — shorter descriptions are a priori more probable. Compression is evidence of having found genuine structure in reality.

The Four-Dimensional Definition

Idea Quality = Truth × Utility × Fitness × Compression A truly good idea scores high on ALL four — multiplication means one zero kills the product

Why Multiplication, Not Addition

We use multiplication deliberately. If any dimension is zero, the product is zero — the idea is not good regardless of how high the other scores are:

• True + useful + compressed but unfit: An idea that cannot spread is a tree falling in an empty forest. It might as well not exist. (Semmelweis's handwashing before it was finally accepted.)

• Fit + useful + compressed but false: A convenient lie. It will dominate until reality catches up — and reality always catches up. (Phlogiston, miasma, geocentrism.)

• True + fit + compressed but useless: An elegant fact with no application. Interesting but inert. It cannot change the world. (Many results in pure mathematics.)

• True + fit + useful but uncompressed: A large body of ad hoc knowledge with no unifying principle. Useful case-by-case but unable to generate predictions beyond what's already known. (Traditional medicine before germ theory unified it.)

The Lifecycle of Ideas

Living, Dying, and Dead

Ideas exist on a spectrum from thriving to extinct:

Thriving ideas: Currently dominant, high adoption, actively generating new work. (Deep learning, CRISPR, blockchain, democracy.)

Mature ideas: Widely accepted but no longer generating excitement. Stable. (Germ theory, heliocentrism, plate tectonics.)

Declining ideas: Losing adherents, challenged by alternatives, accumulating anomalies. (Perhaps: pure free-market economics, classical computing-only approaches to AI.)

Zombie ideas: Should be dead (zero truth) but kept alive by fitness alone — emotional appeal, identity investment, or institutional inertia. (Astrology, homeopathy, various political ideologies.)

Hibernating ideas: True and potentially useful but waiting for their fitness window. (Quantum computing concepts in the 1980s. Many "crackpot" proposals that may be vindicated later.)

Dead ideas: No living adherents, no active transmission, preserved only in historical records. (Phlogiston, geocentrism, Norse mythology as a living religion.)

The idea ecosystem is like a forest. Thriving ideas are the canopy trees — tall, sun-lit, dominant. Mature ideas are the established understory. Declining ideas are trees being shaded out. Zombie ideas are dead trees still standing — held up by structural inertia, providing shelter to parasites, but producing no new growth. Hibernating ideas are seeds in the soil — dormant but potentially explosive when conditions change. And dead ideas are the leaf litter — decomposing, their nutrients recycled into future growth.

Why Dead Ideas Matter

The Compost Heap of Intellectual History

Dead ideas are not simply failures. They often contribute to the ideas that replace them:

Alchemy → Chemistry: Alchemy was false. But alchemists developed distillation, acids, laboratory techniques, and the concept of elements. Chemistry grew from alchemy's compost.

Astrology → Astronomy: Astrologers developed star catalogues, mathematical models of planetary motion, and precision observation techniques. Astronomy inherited the infrastructure.

Paganism → Philosophy: The Greek pantheon's decline created space for philosophical inquiry. Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle emerged precisely as religious certainty weakened.

Vitalism → Biochemistry: The idea that life requires a "vital force" was wrong. But the question "what makes life special?" drove the research that discovered DNA, proteins, and metabolic pathways.

Dead ideas are the failed experiments of intellectual evolution. Each one narrows the search space, demonstrates what doesn't work, and often leaves behind tools, techniques, or partial insights that the successor idea builds upon.

The Rarest Achievement

Ideas That Score Maximum on All Four Dimensions

Scoring high on all four dimensions simultaneously is extraordinarily rare. Most ideas face inherent trade-offs:

Truth vs. Fitness: True ideas are often complex and counterintuitive (low fitness). Fit ideas are often oversimplified (low truth). The rare idea that is both true and simple enough to spread is precious.

Compression vs. Utility: Highly compressed ideas (fundamental physics) often require enormous infrastructure to exploit. Immediately useful ideas (cooking recipes) often have low compression.

Utility vs. Fitness: Useful ideas that require behaviour change (exercise, saving money, handwashing) spread slowly because they impose costs on adopters. Useless ideas that flatter (horoscopes, fad diets) spread fast because they impose no costs.

The ideas that achieve maximum on all four — that are true, useful, fit, and compressed — represent the pinnacle of human intellectual achievement. They are what idea evolution is, in the very long run, converging toward.

A good idea is one that is true enough to survive contact with reality, useful enough to change the world, fit enough to spread across minds, and compressed enough to be remembered forever.
Speculation

Can AI Create Perfectly Good Ideas?

If the four dimensions define idea quality, can AI optimise for all four simultaneously? In principle, AI could:

• Generate ideas with high truth (by testing against data at machine speed)

• Evaluate utility (by simulating applications and consequences)

• Optimise fitness (by tailoring presentation for human psychology)

• Maximise compression (by searching for shorter descriptions that maintain predictive power)

If so, AI could produce ideas that are "optimally good" — scoring maximum on every dimension. Such ideas would be true, immediately useful, irresistibly compelling, and elegantly simple. They would spread like religion and endure like mathematics.

This would be unprecedented. No natural process — neither biological evolution nor cultural evolution — optimises for all four dimensions simultaneously. Each optimises for one or two at the expense of the others. An AI that could break these trade-offs would produce ideas qualitatively unlike anything in human intellectual history.

Whether such multi-dimensional optimisation is possible, or whether the trade-offs are fundamental (impossible to eliminate), is unknown. It's possible that truth and fitness are inherently in tension for any finite mind — that perfectly true ideas are necessarily too complex for perfect fitness. If so, the trade-offs are not a failure of our idea ecosystem but a mathematical inevitability.

Open Questions

  • Are the four dimensions truly independent — or are some mathematically entangled (e.g., does maximum compression guarantee truth via Solomonoff induction)?
  • Should "moral goodness" be a fifth dimension — or is it subsumed under utility (ideas that make the world better are more useful)?
  • Can a false idea ever be "good"? (Noble lies, useful fictions, necessary simplifications — do they score positively on utility despite zero truth?)
  • Is the death of an idea always a loss? Or are some deaths essential — clearing space for better ideas the way forest fires enable new growth?
  • What killed paganism specifically — and does the same mechanism threaten today's dominant ideas?
